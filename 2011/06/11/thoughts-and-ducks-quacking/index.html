Thoughts and Ducks Quacking

Our duck in the backyard

The one thing that stirs up more emotion than most in me has always been the unknown. Does she love me, does she not? Will I miss my flight; lose my job; is it cancer? Seeing a direct connection between emotion, thinking, and speaking offers a straightforward way to understand what chapter 56 means by, One who knows does not speak; one who speaks does not know.(1) Stirring emotion triggers us to think and speak, and ducks to quack. Such emotion overwhelms the ‚Äòcool‚Äô impartial phase of perception that alone is capable of perceiving what chapter 1 describes as‚Ä¶ These two are the same, But diverge in name as they issue forth. On the other hand, emotion cools when, as chapter 16 puts it, I do my utmost to attain emptiness; I hold firmly to stillness. The myriad creatures all rise together and I watch their return. The teaming creatures all return to their separate roots. Here, there is no emotional energy to drive quacking, thinking or speaking. Here, even the unknown is known, and so I can just be a quiet duck.

In other words, the thinking, speaking and quacking are merely symptoms, not causes. Conversely, trusting your thoughts to be true establishes thought as fundamental in its own right instead of simply a symptom of a deeper reality. Trusting what you think to be true leads to difficulty, as chapter 71 cautions, Not to know yet to think that one knows will lead to difficulty. For a long time I assumed chapter 56 was saying that speaking and thinking were a liability somehow, perhaps because it implied not knowing. Happily, I see this more impartially now.
